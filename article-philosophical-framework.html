Hoshi — the philosophical framework

Why a practical, fair contract for ecological governance matters now.

6 min read Governance Ethics Global Commons

We live in an age where global crises such as climate change, biodiversity loss, and geopolitical instability overlap and compete for attention. Yet our tools for cooperation are often inadequate. Such unrest raises a deeper question: how can societies coordinate fairly when nation-states alone cannot manage the global commons?

For centuries, thinkers from Hobbes to Rawls have debated how communities balance self-interest with duty to the wider world. Their ideas provide language for rethinking today’s ecological governance. John Rawls, for example, argued in The Law of Peoples that “Peoples have a duty to assist other Peoples living under unfavourable conditions that prevent their having a just or decent political and social regime.” But in truth, everyone today lives under some form of unfavourable conditions. That means all of us share a duty to protect the systems that make life possible: clean air, stable climates, and secure societies. Is it realistic to imagine an ideal society untouched by the imperfect realities of nation-states?

Philosophers describe the “State of Nature” as life without shared rules: sometimes pictured as cooperative, other times as chaotic and conflict-ridden. If our global order resembles such a state, what responsibilities do we still owe each other? Should they fall mainly to states, to individuals, or to a wider global community? And on a practical level, what can a reasonable person do to serve the common good, and how might such efforts be organised into a fairer society? Neither the ideal society Rawls envisions nor the sovereign authority Hobbes demands seems wholly adequate for today’s ecological and political realities. We live in systems too imperfect for utopias and too plural for absolute authority.

This tension suggests that ordinary people and institutions alike share a responsibility, one not grounded in perfection, but in doing the “best we can do.” Our argument is that this “best we can do society” provides the more complete form of contract that Rawls anticipated but did not fully realise. As a result, this paper argues for what we call a Commonwealth of Peoples: not a utopian “ideal society,” but a practical “best we can do society.” Where Rawls imagines a perfectly just world of cooperating peoples, we propose instead a framework that accepts imperfection, yet equips us to act responsibly toward the global commons. Such a framework enables actors to take meaningful action for the shared ecological commons, without being constrained by the Realpolitik of nation-states. The Commonwealth of Peoples is a more workable contract: one in which imperfect actors can still coordinate fairly, balancing duties to one another and to the ecosystems that sustain us. It focuses on demand-side assurance and investment guidance for private entities, while holding a global perspective. Over time, it could scale universally across all end-use services.
