Commonwealth of People

A practical governance frame for real estate: set a public ecological floor, use a shared carbon price, and turn alarms into enforceable actions with an audit trail.

7 min read Governance Commonwealth Cost of Carbon

The problem in one screen

  • Many hands, shared dependencies. Owners, tenants, lenders, FM and vendors all “do their bit,” but choices don’t add up across contracts.
  • Coordination failure. Carbon, comfort and grid stability are shared resources; without common rules, local optimisations leave gaps and delays.
  • Private prices, soft promises. Internal carbon prices change; claims aren’t versioned; disputes are about opinion, not evidence.
  • Fairness risk. Decisions feel arbitrary without peer-reviewed standards and due process.

The answer: a shared floor + four instruments

  1. Commonwealth Cost of Carbon (CCC). A public, published carbon price floor referenced in underwriting, budgeting and leases. Carbon becomes non-optional in decisions.
    Effect: a heat-pump retrofit that’s marginal at today’s tariffs becomes NPV-positive once avoided emissions are valued at the CCC—so the decision doesn’t hinge on fuel-price luck.
  2. Assured ecological ledger. Every claim is recorded with baseline, method, factors, license and version. Numbers are inspectable rather than asserted; promises become rely-able assets.
  3. Public alarms & rulings. Breaches raise alarms with shared priority (P1–P4). Rulings specify what “good” looks like and how success is verified (M&V, acceptance criteria). They function as enforceable promise-templates.
  4. Executive function (fairness). Rulings are peer-reviewed, explained in clear terms (baseline, method, factors), commissioned in situ, and escalated through a transparent procedure.

How it shows up in practice

  • In leases. Add: Operating plans and capex appraisal will apply the Commonwealth Cost of Carbon at the prevailing public rate.
  • In appraisal. Model retrofit NPV/IRR with CCC, not only tariff savings; compare bundles by resilience across scenarios.
  • In operations. Alarms (e.g., “P2 electricity overrun, 12-month”) map to rulings with acceptance criteria and M&V; outcomes are written to the ledger.

Three quick use-cases

Gas-heavy office

P2 overrun proposes: “LED + schedule + heat-pump pre-study.” Pack shows CapEx, savings, NPV at CCC, β/sensitivity, comfort risk and tCO₂e; acceptance “≥15% vs modelled baseline; M&V by bills (12m), weather-normalised.”

Tenant fit-out

Lease addendum binds both parties to CCC and the rulings for overheating/plug-loads. Disputes escalate; ledger keeps versions and licenses.

Lender covenant

Green-capex facility references the ledger for method/factor versions and uses alarm priorities to trigger remedial plans instead of generic defaults.

FAQ (brief)

Why a public carbon price? It removes discretion and keeps owners, tenants and lenders on the same floor; internal prices drift and weaken trust.

Who sets it? A public body or delegated association with transparent methods and change-control; the latest rate is published and versioned.

Is this a new reporting system? No. Use your reporting stack; the Commonwealth adds a **governance floor** so actions are priced fairly and proven with M&V.

Quick start

  2. Adopt a rulings library for common alarms (overrun, overheating, air-quality).
  3. Write claims to a simple versioned ledger (even a spreadsheet to start).
