Commonwealth of People

Why real estate needs a different kind of order; a practical frame for governance where choices become decision-grade capital signals.

7 min read Hobbes Commonwealth Cost of Carbon Governance

Plain-English idea

Fragmented tools and private carbon prices won’t protect the shared systems we all rely on (grid stability, breathable air, a liveable climate). A Commonwealth of People is a minimal rule-set we accept so decisions in real estate respect a public ecological floor, fairly and transparently.

The three instruments

1) Commonwealth Cost of Carbon (CCC)

A public, published carbon price floor owned by the commonwealth (not by a firm). Build it into underwriting, budgeting and leases so carbon is not optional in decisions.

2) A shared ecological ledger (assured promises)

Claims like “–6.5 tCO₂e/yr” are recorded with baseline, method, factors, license & version history—so partners and auditors can trust them. Promises become enforceable, not just slides.

3) Public alarms & rulings (with due process)

Breaches (e.g., energy overrun, overheating) raise alarms (P1–P4). Rulings define what “good” looks like and how success is verified (M&V, acceptance criteria). Peer review keeps it fair and explainable.

How it shows up in practice

  • Leases & IC memos: “Operating plans and capex appraisal apply the CCC at the prevailing public rate.” Now tenants, owners and auditors align on the same price floor.
  • Assured promises: Every KPI links to its ledger entry (baseline, method, factors, version). Disputes are about evidence, not opinions.
  • Alarms → Actions → M&V → Ruling: When an alarm fires, you commission a standard response with acceptance criteria and meter-verified proof.

A quick vignette

Mid-year, “P2 Electricity Overrun (12-month window)” triggers. The ruling prescribes schedule optimisation + LED, M&V by bills (12m, weather-normalised), and acceptance “≥15% reduction vs modelled baseline.” The measure is priced with CCC, documented in the ledger, and signed off once the evidence turns green.

Why this is fair (not technocratic)

Rulings are peer-reviewed by professional associations and justified in plain language (baseline, method, factors). Methods are versioned; objections follow a transparent procedure.
